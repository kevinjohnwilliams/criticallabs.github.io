The SCL framework wasn't adapted from a generic AI governance document. It was built by someone who spent years inside NASA writing the first AI certification requirements for human-rated spacecraft — requirements that are now part of NASA's governing software standard.

Founder

Kevin J. Williams

Kevin is a software engineer, AI researcher, and the founder of Safety Critical Labs. His work at NASA Johnson Space Center, through Barrios Technology, produced the first AI certification requirements for human-rated spacecraft — requirements that directly shaped NPR 7150.2D, the governing standard for human spaceflight software across Artemis and commercial crew programs.

That work earned the NASA Silver Bear Award — recognition given for exceptional contribution to human spaceflight programs. Kevin didn't study AI certification from the outside. He was in the room where the requirements were written.

Before NASA, Kevin led a 20-person materials engineering lab at Ford Motor Company, where he deployed deep learning for automated quality decisions in electric motor manufacturing — reducing manual inspection by approximately 90% and earning Ford's Technical Excellence Award. That experience of applying AI to production systems with real quality and safety consequences is the lens through which Safety Critical Labs approaches every assessment.

"I didn't start with AI governance theory. I started with the question of what happens when an AI system is wrong at the wrong moment — and worked backward to what a certifiable standard needs to say."

Why it matters

Authority transfers
from person to institution.

Safety Critical Labs is at an early stage where authority rests on the founder's direct credentials — NPR 7150.2D, the Silver Bear Award, NASA JSC, Ford Motor Company. That's enough to support the first customers.

The goal is to transfer that credibility from the person to the institution: through an advisory board of domain experts, formal publication of the framework, regulatory engagement, and ultimately accreditation through ANAB or A2LA under ISO/IEC 17065.

◆ Domain-first methodology. Requirements developed from inside the operational environment — not adapted from a governance template by someone who has never worked in safety-critical systems.
◆ Published standard. Framework v1.0 is on GitHub with a citable DOI. The assessor who wrote the framework is the assessor conducting the certification.
◆ Regulatory record. Engaging with FAA, FDA, and DoD AI policy processes as they develop. First-mover presence in regulatory records is how standards get adopted.
◆ Accreditation path. ISO/IEC 17065 gap assessment is part of the company roadmap. The long game is a regulatory instrument, not just a credible third party.
